our grader is fundamentally broken, and here is the defect in precise terms.
This grading system does not assess whether a student reasoned correctly. It assesses whether the student retrieved a pre-blessed vocabulary token, and it does so while penalizing answers for their format rather than their substance. The evidence is in the grader's own output. A student shown a case where Jordan believes a false campus post, and asked to classify the belief, wrote "ERROR." The grader marked it wrong and demanded "false belief"—while admitting the student "gestures at something being wrong." But a belief that is in error is a false belief; "ERROR" and "false belief" pick out the identical epistemic state. The grader found a lexical mismatch and relabeled it a conceptual failure. This is doubly damning in a critical thinking course, because the defect is itself the exact fallacy the course purports to teach: treating "the student didn't say the magic word" as equivalent to "the student doesn't understand the concept" is a non sequitur. A course that grades this way violates its own subject matter on every item, rewarding the brown-noser who memorized the glossary and failing the student who can actually think.
Three things must change, and they are mandatory, not cosmetic. First, grade the phenomenon, not the label. Every grading call must receive the exact question stem and be instructed: Award full credit when the student correctly picks out the concept being tested, regardless of terminology. "ERROR," "false belief," "a mistaken belief," "he believes something untrue" are all correct for a false belief; "due diligence," "verification," "checking the source" are all correct for that concept. Deduct only when the student identifies the wrong phenomenon or reasons invalidly—never for non-canonical wording. Second, grade substance, not format. It does not matter whether the student answers in complete sentences, bullet points, sentence fragments, shorthand, or a single clause. If the substance is present and correct, it earns full credit. A fragment that nails the concept beats a polished paragraph that misses it. The grader must never deduct for terseness, lack of prose, missing transitions, or any stylistic property whatsoever; style is not the variable under test. Third, eliminate every pure-memorization question from the bank. No item may have the form "what is the name for X," "what is the term for Y," "this is an example of ___," or any stem whose answer is a retrievable label. Those questions test recall, not reasoning, and they are the root of the whole disease—they are the only kind of question a vocabulary-matching grader can even pose. Replace each one with an item that forces an operation: surface the hidden assumption in this argument; give the strongest objection to this claim; explain why this inference fails; identify what evidence would change the conclusion. A question the student can pass by recalling a word is, by definition, not a critical thinking question. Until all three are fixed, this is not a reasoning course. It is a vocabulary loyalty test wearing the robe of rigor, and it fails precisely the students it exists to reward.
